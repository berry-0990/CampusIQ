Section 01

The Problem

Students don't have one reliable place for academic info. So they turn to ChatGPT — which has never seen their syllabus.

📂

Information is scattered

Syllabi, notes, rubrics, and announcements live on different platforms — email, LMS, Google Drive, and more. Students waste hours searching through all of them.

🤖

AI gives confident wrong answers

General AI tools like ChatGPT were never trained on your campus documents. They generate fluent, convincing answers — but they can be completely wrong about course-specific details.

📅

Missed deadlines & wrong rubrics

Students misread submission dates, follow incorrect formatting rules, or prepare for exams using AI summaries that quietly contain errors. The consequences are real.

🚫

No trusted AI alternative

There's currently no tool that gives students AI-speed answers grounded in actual campus resources. Students are forced to choose between speed and accuracy.

AI hallucination isn't an occasional bug — it's a structural property of how language models work. They don't "know" facts; they predict likely text. Without retrieval, no amount of fine-tuning fully solves this. CampusIQ is built to close that gap.

Section 02

The Solution

A RAG system that searches verified campus documents first — and only then generates a response, strictly grounded in what it found.

CampusIQ is a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) system built for academic environments. Rather than relying on what an AI model "thinks it knows," CampusIQ first searches a curated database of campus-specific documents, retrieves the most relevant content, and only then generates a response — constrained entirely to that retrieved evidence.

If the answer isn't in the documents, the system says so — clearly and explicitly — instead of making something up.

Why RAG reduces hallucinations: Language models hallucinate because they simulate recall rather than perform it — they generate probable text, not verified facts. RAG fixes this by removing the model from the role of fact-store entirely. The document corpus holds the knowledge; the LLM only reasons over what retrieval hands it. That architectural separation is what makes responses trustworthy.

Section 03

How It Works

From raw campus documents to a cited, grounded answer — in one seamless pipeline.

01

Ingest Campus Documents

Syllabi, lecture notes, assignment briefs, lab manuals, LMS announcements, and past exam papers are collected as PDFs, DOCX files, or HTML pages. PyMuPDF handles extraction and cleaning.

PDF · DOCX · HTML
02

Semantic Chunking

Documents are split into meaningful chunks of 300–500 tokens, with a 50-token overlap between adjacent chunks so no information is lost at boundaries.

300–500 tok · 50 overlap
03

Generate Embeddings

Each chunk is converted into a numerical vector using BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5 — one of the best open-source embedding models available. These vectors capture the meaning of text, not just keywords.

bge-large-en-v1.5
04

Store in Vector Database

Embeddings are stored in ChromaDB, organized per course and department. The database can scale to Pinecone for production without changing any pipeline code.

ChromaDB → Pinecone
05

Query Rewriting with HyDE

When a student asks a question, HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embedding) rewrites it into an ideal answer form before embedding — dramatically improving retrieval quality on vague or poorly phrased questions.

HyDE Rewrite
06

Retrieve Top-5 Matches

Hybrid retrieval combines dense vector search with sparse BM25 keyword matching. The top 5 most relevant document chunks are selected using cosine similarity scores.

Cosine Similarity · BM25
07

Generate a Cited Response

The LLM receives only the retrieved chunks as context. It generates a response grounded strictly in those documents, and includes a direct source citation so students can verify what they're told.

Source-Cited Output
Section 04

Feasibility at a Glance

CampusIQ is built on open-source tooling with low infrastructure cost and a clear path to production.

Factor Rating Details
Data Availability Medium Most campuses already have digitized materials on Moodle, Canvas, or Google Classroom. The effort is structured collection and faculty agreements — not creating content from scratch.
Technical Complexity Medium RAG is a well-documented paradigm with mature tooling (LangChain, LlamaIndex). A working prototype with core retrieval and generation is achievable in 2–3 weeks by a small Python-familiar team.
Infrastructure Cost Low BAAI/bge and ChromaDB are fully open-source. GPT-4o-mini costs ~$0.15 per million tokens. Switching to self-hosted Mistral-7B brings operational cost to near zero.
Scalability High Vector databases scale horizontally with namespacing per course or department. Migration from ChromaDB to Pinecone requires no pipeline changes. The system grows with the data.
Real-World Impact High Directly and measurably reduces academic misinformation. Impact is quantifiable: response accuracy vs. baseline LLM, query resolution rate, time saved, and faculty-reported trust scores.
Section 05

Challenges & Mitigations

Real risks, honestly addressed — with concrete architectural solutions for each.

01
Stale or Outdated Documents
If an old version of a syllabus or rubric stays indexed, students receive formally correct but factually wrong answers — the exact failure mode the system is meant to prevent, now with an air of authority behind it.
Fix: Document versioning with upload timestamps and deprecation flags. When faculty upload a new file, older versions are automatically marked superseded. Responses drawn from older documents display a visible staleness warning.
02
Privacy & Data Security
Student queries may contain personal context — grades, disputes, personal circumstances. Sending these to a third-party API raises genuine FERPA compliance and data residency concerns at institutional scale.
Fix: The architecture is designed for full self-hosting using Mistral-7B on campus infrastructure — no data leaves institutional servers. Role-based access controls restrict document visibility to enrolled courses only. Query anonymization is applied at the API gateway before any processing.
03
Vague or Ambiguous Student Queries
Students rarely phrase questions precisely — "what about the project submission?" gives the retrieval system very little to work with, producing poor matches and ultimately unhelpful responses.
Fix: Hybrid retrieval (dense vector search + sparse BM25) improves recall across both semantic and keyword-specific queries. HyDE rewrites the student's question into an ideal answer form before embedding — significantly improving retrieval on vague inputs. A clarification prompt is triggered automatically when similarity scores fall below a confidence threshold.
Section 06

How CampusIQ Compares

NotebookLM proved document-grounded AI works. CampusIQ takes that idea to institutional scale.

Google NotebookLM

Grounds Gemini responses in user-uploaded documents using the same RAG philosophy. It demonstrated that document-constrained generation is technically viable and genuinely valuable — users loved getting accurate, cited answers.

What CampusIQ Adds

NotebookLM is a personal productivity tool built for individual notes. CampusIQ is institutional infrastructure — multi-tenant, multi-course, LMS-integrated, FERPA-conscious, and role-aware. Built for an entire campus, not one person.

Research Foundation — Lewis et al. (2020), "Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks" (NeurIPS). This foundational paper demonstrated that retrieval-augmented models substantially outperform closed-book LLMs on factual accuracy benchmarks — the empirical basis for why RAG is the right architectural choice here.

Section 09

Conclusion

CampusIQ applies a technically proven approach — Retrieval-Augmented Generation — to a problem that is genuinely unsolved in most academic environments. Students currently have to choose between slow manual searching and fast but unreliable AI. This system offers a third option: AI that is both fast and grounded in verified sources.

The architecture is practical. The stack is low-cost and open-source. A working prototype is realistic within weeks, and the system scales naturally as more campus documents are added. Every response comes with a citation, which means students can verify what they're told — and that changes the relationship between AI tools and academic trust, fundamentally.

That's what makes CampusIQ worth building.
